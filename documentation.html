Design Process

The design of my visualization was fairly simple. Unfortunately, the time it took to learn and implement D3 took away from the time I was able to invest in the creativity of the visualization. My process started with the basics of having an SVG on the page that represented Dr. John Snow’s cholera map. From there I decided I wanted to visualize the genders by color and the age groups by symbols. After getting the map recreated, I added the line graph that showed the deaths per day. I made the decision to align the line graph to the right of the map so that it would be easy to see the day filter applied to the map. I then decided to simply use bar charts to visualize the age and gender data. Once again, I would have preferred to spend more time to make the visualization a little more intricate, but the ramp up speed for learning D3 as well as the tediousness nature of manually creating SVGs using D3 took away from being more creative with the visualization. After getting the charts created, I thought that it would be interesting to add an age and gender filter. The filters are applied to all visualizations (except the deaths/day line graph) and will work with the day filter as well. In retrospect, it would have been nice to have the age and gender filters work on the timeline graph, but the limited development time prevented the ability to add that feature. Lastly, the map legend was created to give the user a resource to understand the meaning of the markings on the map, and the zoom/pan functionality was implemented to allow a better view of what was happening around the map.

Rationale

My visualization is not flashy. I intentionally did not want to overcomplicate the data by adding too much color or overloaded information. However, there are a few things worth noting in my rationale. First, and probably most interesting, is the way I encoded the age and gender data on the map. As explained in the design process, I encoded the gender data with colors (shades of blue and pink) and the ages with symbols. I chose colors for gender since male and female already have the colors associated with them (infants wear blue if male and pink if female). The symbols were designated in order to more quickly see metadata about the deaths on the map. Admittedly, the symbols are difficult to decipher if the map isn’t filtered, but it is still more telling than using a circle. Another other major design choice was the alignment of the charts. I spent so much time learning and implement functionality that creativity took a back seat to functionality. That is why the visualization is quite blocky and boring. The choice to put the legend and filters at the top of the page was so that it is obvious that filtering is a functionality of the visualization. The last rationale worth explaining is why I put the ’about’ page in the navbar. This decision was made because of the limited space for the visualization, and the professor’s desire to have all of the graphs in view without scrolling. Nonetheless, I preferred the lack of clutter that words would have put in the visualization, and I think it makes the site feel more robust.

Discoveries by Visualization

As I have alluded to many times, almost the entirety of my time was spent developing because of the scope of the project requirements was so vast. The being said, I did appreciate the how the metadata distinguished deaths. In the first included screen shot you can see how the timeline give an idea of the lifecycle of the disease. Another interesting observation was the higher deaths in the first and last age brackets. This intuitively makes sense considering these ages typically have weaker immune systems and succumb to disease before other age groups. Of course, these observations are aside from the original conclusion that John Snow had, that there is a correlation of deaths to the center pump. That correlation is just as obvious in my visualization, if not more so.
